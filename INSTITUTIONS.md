# Rwanda Criminal Intelligence Management System (RCIMS)
## Plain-Language Guide — Who Uses It, What They Do, and Why It Matters

---

## What Is This System?

Before RCIMS existed, Rwanda's security institutions — the police, the intelligence service, the army, the prisons — each kept their own separate records. If the Rwanda National Police arrested a suspect, the Rwanda Investigation Bureau might not know about it. If NISS flagged a dangerous individual, the border officer checking passports at Gatuna might have no idea. Information sat in filing cabinets, spreadsheets, and siloed databases that did not talk to each other.

**RCIMS changes that.** It is a single, secure digital platform where all of Rwanda's key security institutions share criminal intelligence in real time. A record created by one institution is instantly visible to the others — within the boundaries of what each institution is allowed to see.

The result: faster decisions, fewer dangerous people slipping through the gaps, and a complete trail of who did what and when — so there is accountability at every level.

---

## The Seven Institutions — What They Do and How RCIMS Helps Them

---

### 1. NISS — National Intelligence and Security Service

**Who they are:**
NISS is Rwanda's national intelligence authority — the institution at the top of the security hierarchy. They do not patrol streets or run prisons. Their job is to see the full picture: who are the threats, where are they, what are they doing, and how do all the pieces connect across the country and across borders.

**What they do on the system:**

- They can see everything — every suspect, every case, every alert, across all institutions at the same time.
- They build and maintain the national suspect database. When a new dangerous individual is identified, NISS creates their profile, classifies the threat level, and makes it visible to all relevant institutions.
- They monitor the country's CCTV network — knowing which cameras are online, which are offline, and what they are capturing.
- They track where security personnel are operating in real time, giving them a live picture of the field.
- They maintain relationships with international partners (Interpol, foreign intelligence agencies) and can query or share intelligence with those partners.
- The NISS Director can, in a declared national security emergency, lock down the entire system with a single command — immediately cutting access for all non-NISS users until the threat passes.
- They maintain the audit log — a permanent, tamper-proof record of every action taken on the system by anyone.

**Why this matters:**
Before RCIMS, connecting the dots between a suspect spotted at the border, a case being investigated in Kigali, and an Interpol alert from Nairobi required phone calls, emails, and days of waiting. Now NISS sees all of it on one screen, in seconds.

> **Key point:** NISS is the brain of the system. They do not need to be everywhere physically because the system brings all the information to them.

---

### 2. RNP — Rwanda National Police

**Who they are:**
The Rwanda National Police are the frontline law enforcement institution. Officers are on the streets every day — responding to crime, conducting investigations, issuing warrants, and catching wanted individuals.

**What they do on the system:**

- **Commanders and detectives** can search the suspect database, open and manage criminal cases, and issue arrest warrants directly on the system. When a warrant is issued, every other institution sees it immediately.
- They maintain the national wanted persons list. If someone is on this list, border officers, patrol units, and correction facilities are all automatically notified.
- Detectives can query Interpol to check if a suspect has an international criminal record.
- They can see where their patrol officers are operating on a live map, and coordinate deployments in real time.
- **Patrol officers** in the field use a mobile app to scan National IDs. The system instantly tells the officer whether the person in front of them has a criminal record — without the officer needing to call a dispatcher or wait for a radio response.

**Why this matters:**
An RNP detective working a case in Musanze used to have no easy way of knowing whether the same suspect was already being investigated by RIB in Kigali, or whether they were already flagged by NISS. Now the information is shared automatically, so investigations do not overlap and suspects cannot exploit the gaps between institutions.

> **Key point:** RNP gets instant answers in the field and coordinates investigations across the country without paperwork delays.

---

### 3. RIB — Rwanda Investigation Bureau

**Who they are:**
The Rwanda Investigation Bureau handles serious and complex criminal investigations — corruption, financial crime, organized crime, and cases that require deep, long-running investigative work. RIB operates independently from the police to ensure impartiality in high-profile cases.

**What they do on the system:**

- **Investigators** open and manage cases, build suspect profiles with supporting evidence, and document everything — witnesses, events, connections between suspects — in a structured way that can be used in court.
- They can see whether a suspect they are investigating is already known to RNP or NISS, preventing duplicated effort and revealing connections the investigator might not have known about.
- They file intelligence reports from the field — documenting what they observed, where, and when — so the information is captured and shared in real time.
- **Analysts** support investigators by reviewing intelligence, monitoring watchlists, and identifying patterns across multiple cases without needing to access the full case files themselves.

**Why this matters:**
Complex investigations often span months. Before RCIMS, an RIB investigator might spend weeks building a picture of a suspect's network, only to discover late in the process that RNP already had key information. Now both institutions see the same data from day one.

> **Key point:** RIB can build stronger, faster cases because they work with the full national picture — not just what their own team has collected.

---

### 4. RDF — Rwanda Defence Force

**Who they are:**
The Rwanda Defence Force protects national borders and handles threats to national security that go beyond normal policing. At borders and at sensitive national sites, RDF personnel are the first line of defence against infiltration and illegal entry.

**What they do on the system:**

- **Commanders** can view suspects flagged by NISS or RNP and check whether anyone crossing a border point is on the national watchlist or has an Interpol record.
- **Border officers** scan the National IDs of people crossing border points. The system immediately tells them whether that person is wanted, flagged, or has a criminal record — in seconds, without radio calls.
- Border posts are mapped on a live border map, showing where personnel are deployed and flagging any unusual crossing activity.
- Cameras at border crossing points feed into the system so commanders can monitor crossings remotely.

**Why this matters:**
A wanted suspect who knows that RNP is looking for them in Kigali might try to cross at a small border post, assuming the information has not reached that officer yet. With RCIMS, the border officer scanning IDs has the same information as the RNP detective pursuing the case — the suspect cannot outrun the system.

> **Key point:** RDF closes the border gap — wanted individuals can no longer escape by moving to a crossing point that "doesn't know" about them.

---

### 5. RCS — Rwanda Correctional Service

**Who they are:**
The Rwanda Correctional Service runs the country's prisons and correctional facilities. Their job is to hold convicted persons and remand prisoners securely, manage their rehabilitation, and process court-ordered changes to their custody — transfers, sentence completions, early release decisions.

**What they do on the system:**

- **Superintendents and officers** maintain a live register of every inmate: which facility they are in, their custody status, when their next review is, and what their sentence record looks like.
- When a court issues a correction order — a transfer, a sentence adjustment, a release — RCS records it on the system immediately, and institutions like NISS and RNP can see the updated status.
- They can look up whether a person being processed into their facility is already known to other institutions — their criminal history, their case status, any special handling instructions.
- Upcoming review dates are flagged automatically so that no prisoner's review is missed due to administrative oversight.

**Why this matters:**
Before RCIMS, a prisoner could theoretically be released from one facility while an active arrest warrant from a different institution was sitting in someone else's inbox, unactioned. Now the warrant is visible to the facility at the moment of processing — nothing falls through the cracks.

> **Key point:** RCS has a complete and live picture of every person in custody, and the rest of the security system knows the moment custody status changes.

---

### 6. Irondo — Community Patrol Units

**Who they are:**
Irondo is Rwanda's community policing programme — unarmed volunteers and low-level community security officers who operate at the cell and sector level. They are the eyes and ears at the neighbourhood level, the people who know their community and notice when something is wrong.

**What they do on the system:**

- Irondo patrol officers use a mobile app to check a person's National ID on the spot — during a community check, at a local event, or when something seems suspicious.
- The system gives them a simple answer: **this person has a criminal record** or **this person is clear**. That is all they see — no name, no case details, no profile. Just the result.
- If a criminal record is found, the system automatically captures the GPS location of the check and sends an alert to RNP — so police officers can respond immediately to the right location.
- If a person is clean, nothing is recorded about them. Their privacy is fully protected.

**Why this matters:**
Irondo officers cannot be expected to memorise tens of thousands of wanted individuals. With RCIMS, every community-level patrol officer carries the entire national wanted database in their pocket — in a form they can actually use, without needing to understand police systems or criminal databases.

> **Key point:** Irondo turns every community checkpoint into an intelligent verification point. A wanted fugitive cannot blend into a neighbourhood because even the local patrol volunteer can identify them in seconds.

---

### 7. DASSO — District Administration Security Support Officers

**Who they are:**
DASSO officers are security personnel attached to district and sector administration offices — the people responsible for security at government premises, public offices, and administrative gatherings. They are not police officers but they are part of the formal security structure.

**What they do on the system:**

- Exactly the same as Irondo: they use the mobile app to scan IDs at administrative checkpoints — when people enter government offices, at community meetings, at district-level events.
- They get the same binary result: record found or all clear. No sensitive details are shown.
- Any record-found event triggers an automatic alert to RNP and captures the location.

**Why this matters:**
Government offices and public events are high-value targets for individuals trying to operate undetected in official spaces. DASSO officers extend the reach of the verification network into spaces that traditional policing cannot cover continuously.

> **Key point:** DASSO ensures that even administrative spaces are not blind spots in the national security picture.

## How These Institutions Work Together

The real power of RCIMS is not what any single institution can do — it is what happens when all seven are connected.

Here is a realistic example of how the system works across institutions:

1. An **Irondo patrol officer** scans an ID at a neighbourhood checkpoint. The system quietly returns: record found.
2. An **automatic alert** is sent to the nearest **RNP** patrol unit with the GPS location.
3. The RNP officer arriving on scene can see in the app that the individual is linked to an active case being investigated by **RIB**.
4. RIB investigators are notified in real time and can coordinate the arrest.
5. **NISS** sees the full chain of events in the audit log and can flag whether the individual has any international connections.
6. If the individual is taken into custody, **RCS** receives their record automatically and opens an inmate file.
7. **RDF** border alerts are updated so that if anyone connected to this individual tries to cross a border, they are flagged immediately.

This entire sequence — from neighbourhood checkpoint to coordinated arrest to custody record — happens in minutes, with every institution seeing what they need to see.

Without RCIMS, the same sequence would require phone calls between institutions, manual record lookups, physical paperwork transfers, and could take days — during which the suspect may disappear.

## Summary — What RCIMS Solves

| Problem Before RCIMS | How RCIMS Solves It |
|---|---|
| Each institution kept its own separate records | One shared platform — all institutions see relevant information instantly |
| A wanted person could cross a border before the alert reached the border post | Border officers have live access to the same national watchlist as investigators |
| Community patrol had no reliable way to identify dangerous individuals | Mobile ID scanner gives patrol officers instant yes/no results |
| A suspect could be investigated twice by different institutions without either knowing | Investigators see if a suspect already has a case with another institution |
| Prisoners could be released while active warrants existed in another institution's paperwork | Custody changes are visible to all relevant institutions in real time |
| No single institution could see the full national security picture | NISS has a live command view of the entire country's security activity |
| No permanent record of who accessed what information and when | Every action is recorded in a tamper-proof audit log |

## Who Can See What — Simple Version

| What kind of information | Who can see it |
|---|---|
| Full suspect profiles, case details, intelligence reports | NISS, RNP (investigators), RIB, RDF (commanders) |
| Whether a person has a criminal record (yes/no only) | Everyone, including Irondo and DASSO patrol officers |
| Where security officers are located right now | NISS (everyone), RNP (their own patrol), RDF (border positions) |
| Prison and custody records | NISS, RNP, RIB (read), RCS (manage) |
| International criminal records and Interpol alerts | NISS, RNP, RIB, RDF commanders |
| The full audit trail of all system activity | NISS only |
| The ability to lock down the entire system in an emergency | NISS Director only (requires two Directors to confirm) |
